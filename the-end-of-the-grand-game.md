# The End of the Grand Game

*Bobby Bress · source spine for the 16/15 and Hirst articles · draft*

---

## Prefatory note

This is the substructure. It is the frame I built before I wrote a word about a canvas signed 16/15, and it is the frame the Hirst article inherits. Everything downstream — the Brancusi edition that took ninety years to fill, the Banksy print that sold below its own prior comp, the catalog raisonné that stops at 2005 — is a symptom. This is the disease. Read this first and the auction results stop being a market story and start being what they are: the worked example of an economic law the discipline that was supposed to model it left out.

I am a one-man think tank working the grocer's grammar — pre-theoretical, evidence-based knowledge of what things actually cost. I have an Econ-math degree and I read the canonical texts under a world-literature umbrella, on purpose, to marry the anglophone to the continental. I never found the model that closes the loop. It took me until the 2010s to find the piece that was missing, and a while longer to see that its absence was not an accident of my education. It was a structural omission in the field. This essay is about that omission and what got built in the space it left.

---

## I. Two layers

There are two layers in everything that follows, and they jam together if you don't keep them apart.

The first layer is a point of view: the extractive capitalist's. Not a villain — a vantage. The late-twentieth-century allocator who treats artistic production exactly the way a portfolio treats its riskiest venture sleeve. From that seat, art is simply the most unstable production class there is: a brutal failure rate, the occasional civilization-altering payoff, and a vast middle that ends as footnotes in books nobody reads. This is not a hostile way to look at art. It is the *honest* way to look at it if your only mandate is to maximize the value of your personal holdings while funding the least stable of all ventures.

The second layer is the specific maneuver that gets run on top of that vantage in the British and global art markets across 2000–2020. The two-pronged launch. The constructed superstar slate. The keystone contracted identities. The edition-as-headcount apparatus dressed as fine-art scarcity. That is the layer the 16/15 article and the Hirst article actually litigate.

The seam between the two layers is the whole game. Layer one is a lens anyone can pick up; held as a lens it accuses no one. Layer two is a set of named entities doing named things. The mistake — mine, for years, and the culture's, still — is to argue layer two without layer one underneath it, because without the lens the maneuver looks like ordinary commerce, and with the lens it looks like exactly what it is.

## II. The law layer one runs on

Begin with the cruelty that organizes the venture sleeve: **technical superiority does not predict captured value.** The record is unambiguous and it has nothing to do with art.

Betamax was the better format and it lost to VHS. The operating system that became the most valuable software franchise on earth was built on a pirated predecessor, while Apple — which made real money — made it by making real things, not by owning the cleanest code. The pattern repeats because the legal code of any given moment was never written to stop the move the winner actually pulled. Law lags the maneuver. The gap between what the law anticipated and what the operator did is precisely where the value gets captured, and the code only forms *after* — too late for the holding it would have protected. The worst actors frequently capture the most, not despite the rules but through the seams in rules that never imagined them.

That is layer one's first law. Here is its second, and it is the one the economics profession does not carry: **a market is not supply and demand in the abstract. It is production for worldly ends, and poor management produces great excess that has to be disposed of.**

This is the part Marx leaves out — or, more precisely, the part that was left out of Marx. Marx gives you production and the extraction of surplus value. He treats excess as a symptom to be redistributed. What he does not give you is *expenditure as a structural necessity* — the law that every working system produces more energy than it can absorb, that the surplus must go somewhere, and that a civilization is defined by *how* it spends the part it cannot keep. Gloriously: gift, festival, monument, art, sacrifice. Or catastrophically: war.

Bataille named it the accursed share. The accursed share is the missing variable. An economy with no model for smoothing its own excess through honest expenditure does not avoid the expenditure — it cannot, the excess is real — it simply expends it badly, which is to say catastrophically, and then has no language to explain to itself what happened. There is no divine sanction for the disposal. So the disposal gets rationalized, in the absence of any better account, as accumulation-as-individual-right. Useful enough in the build phase. Fatal at the moment centralization hoards the essential resources at the point of service and the system collapses with no buffer, because the buffer was the expenditure it refused to make on purpose and made by accident instead.

I went looking for that variable in the canonical economic models. It is not there. I found it in Bataille, in the 2010s, in a literature-and-philosophy quarantine, while the economics departments ran equilibrium models with no slot for it. That quarantine is not a footnote. It is the reason no one in the institutional room approaching Y2K could read the art-market pump as expenditure-malfunction in real time. The framework that would have named it had been kept in the wrong building.

## III. The generation that ate the brown acid and never came down

The boomers are layer one's second law made flesh.

They were the first Western generation at scale never to know poverty. So they bought the idea of radical accumulation as an individual right and then — the genuinely poisonous move — they sold it back to everyone else as a universal law, on infomercials, and then weaponized the *fear* of insecurity to harvest the same population's datasets under the banner of companies that care. They celebrated accumulation without collapse. A floor show by the band on the Titanic, with none of the injuries and none of the lessons that the injuries teach.

And the lessons were the point. The injuries are what would have inoculated the West against the return of war to its own doorstep in the 2020s. A culture that had reckoned honestly with its own excess — that had a working theory of expenditure — would have recognized the symptoms. Instead the unreckoned excess returned at the largest available scale, as it always does when there is no other channel for it: Russia spending into Afghanistan, then Syria, then Ukraine, expenditure it should have kept to itself; and the flattening of a strip where two million people lived for the security of a state, after the taking of roughly twelve hundred lives and two hundred and fifty hostages — the inverse of Masada, and I will say plainly as a bar-mitzvah'd Jew that I do not understand what has become of my people in it. That is not a digression from the economic argument. It is the economic argument at its terminal scale: excess that is not expended gloriously is expended catastrophically, and a civilization with no model for the difference cannot tell which one it is doing while it does it.

The 1920s and 1930s understood this in a way we have unlearned. The futurists, Bataille and André Masson and the Acéphale circle — the headless figure, the men who imagined cutting off their own heads to do their part to balance excess against felicitous oversupply. They knew the *balancing* of excess was itself the work. They were doing economics. We filed them under art and stopped reading.

## IV. The control cases: what honest production looks like

Before naming the maneuver, name the controls — the artists who tell you what the real thing is, so the counterfeit is legible against them. Not all of the twenty-first-century brands are the same kind of thing, and the difference is exactly layer one's distinction between sustainable expenditure and extraction. The technique will not tell you which is which. The intent, the scale, and the disposal logic will.

**Richter is the control for the technique question.** He makes his own work — and "his own work" still meant ten-plus people refining paint, straining it through muslin to declump the pigment, testing materials every which way, an apparatus underneath the hand. The Tate's own conservator has documented it: the squeegee, the drying-rate knowledge, the deliberate musing over an under-painting that ninety percent of will be permanently buried, the chance-and-craft of a man dragging Perspex across a tacky surface and trusting his materials. He used an *excess of labor to co-produce in limited, sustainable quantities* as Germany rebounded and pulled Europe back into the Western fold. That is not extraction. And note the trap it sets: Richter's investors were not averse to perfecting the squeegee painting as a fine-art instrument, in the same era, alongside Hirst's spin painting. The two gestures are formal cousins. One strand is sustainable co-production; the other is the extractive pump. Same perfected mechanical gesture; opposite economies. **The squeegee and the spin are cousins. Richter and the Hirst apparatus are opposites.** Anyone who reads the technique as the tell has already lost the thread.

**Heizer is the control for the value question.** He came out of the sixties not into greed-is-good but into fifty-five years of its opposite — motorcycle rings carved into the California desert, *Double Negative*, *City* — work whose entire proposition is duration without transaction. Left untouched it lasts centuries, like the sailing stones on the Racetrack Playa in Death Valley: rocks that slid across the dry lakebed when the last ice age released them and then stopped, leaving a track that is real, permanent, and *short*. That is the image. Honest expenditure leaves a true mark and makes no claim to have traveled further than it did. The production-brand object does the inverse: it claims an enormous distance — scarcity, edition, appreciation curve — that it never actually traveled. The desert cannot be marked to market. Heizer is what art looks like when it refuses to be liquid.

The sixties sorted into three. The acid-heads who saw too little or too much went to the extremes and collapsed — Jerry Rubin the terminal specimen, the Yippie who walked off the barricade and onto the trading floor, living proof the decade's energy could be reabsorbed into pure accumulation without a residue. The extremes failed. **The middle made the history that should have legs.** Ed Ruscha made the only move left in the two-dimensional epoch: he took the words on the page — the printed mark, the type, the etching lineage that *began* information production and runs straight through to the chip — and turned them back into really nice pictures. He closed the loop. Kienholz, Burden, Smithson, the Bechers, David Mach, Goldsworthy — the others working the real middle. And then the hard claim, which I hold as fact and as opinion at once: **from roughly 1980 forward, in the West, progress froze.**

Not because the genius pool changed. Because 1980 is approximately when the production-brand model became the dominant allocation target, and once capital selects for tradability rather than for the singular act, the cauldron stops being funded. You do not get fewer geniuses. You get fewer *remembered* artists, because the human support infrastructure that converts a singular act into a durable public mark got rerouted into manufacturing scarcity for instruments. The freeze is not aesthetic. It is an allocation consequence. The Death Valley rocks remember the ice age. An edition of twenty-five remembers nothing, because it never happened the way the number says it did.

## V. The maneuver: the two-pronged launch

Now layer two, built on the controls.

Damien Hirst is not the apex of the architecture. He is a role-holder — the not-secret Saatchi, the visible operator of the UK faction — while the apex is the capital: Saatchi, the Saatchi family, and the silent partners with enough liquidity to fund the Young British Artists' launch across the 1990s. Hirst runs the floor. The capital and the design sit above him. Which means the Goldsmiths studio model, the spot and spin and formaldehyde production lines, the financial scaling that could absorb the venture-sleeve capital and convert it into inventory — all of it was the *research and development*. The YBA launch was the proof of concept. It taught the backers the model.

The model was then deployed in a two-pronged launch across two deliberately different markets.

**Prong one is the overclass prong** — the generational-wealth instrument. The industrial-grade production-artist identity brand, engineered to be the twenty-first century's answer to the Impressionist cohort that became the load-bearing asset class for English-language wealth transfer across the twentieth. Not artists in the arte-povera sense — the cauldron, the singular act, the line that runs from Blake forward — but *brands* with a production line, an edition apparatus, and a balance sheet. A coordinated international slate, roughly one keystone per major market: Koons as the American precedent, Hirst for the UK, then carried forward and joined by Murakami in Japan, Ai Weiwei in China, Fairey and KAWS in the US, Banksy in the UK, Invader in France. A constructed superstar tier. And the historical rhyme is Klimt: the genuinely best painter of the 1900 turn shoved behind enemy lines by the century's wars, emerging late and triumphant, second in price only to a propped-up *Salvator Mundi* — which is the whole lesson in one comparison. The market's canon and the aesthetic canon are not the same canon. The asset-class machine selects for tradability, not for the act. Prong one does not compete with the cauldron. It *contains* it — carries the corpse of arte povera inside the brand machine where it can be neutralized.

**Prong two is the underclass prong** — the street-art movement, roughly 2000–2010, addressed at an entirely different market. Where the 1900 turn fed on domestic marginal culture, the global-economy version mainstreams the global underclass: third-world and underclass artists entering the asset structure the way Black artists did in music and Jewish artists did in film and television and music across the twentieth. Street art is the channel through which the underclass is brought into the structure, and "Banksy" is the keystone contracted identity subbed into that prong the way Hirst is the keystone of prong one. Same model, two prongs, two class-addressed markets, one set of backers with the liquidity to run both.

The genuine sort within the slate then follows layer one exactly. Ai Weiwei's sunflower seeds, the salvaged-trawler Buddha — real expenditure statements, honest about being expenditure, on the right side of the Bataille ledger and cool as hell. Matthew Barney wrote himself out of the business entirely and pushed heavy-metal sculpture a universe past Serra, which is precisely why those masses will eventually be recycled — they return to the material economy rather than pretending to permanence. KAWS is transparent: no pretense, you can see straight through to the brand. The point of the sort is not taste. It is that only *some* of these objects are pretending that mechanical replication carries closed-edition collectable scarcity, and that pretense is the layer-one extraction maneuver wearing layer-two clothes.

## VI. They looted the wrong medium

Here is the correction that turns the exposé into something larger, and it is the one I am most sure of.

I love pictures and sculpture. I am not nostalgic for them as *the* store of value. I am being ruthless about where the value actually carried. The motherland of information technology — the anglophone world that built the substrate — carried itself forward *artistically* not through decorative objects but through the songbooks, the music, the performance, the film, the television, the animation. Those catalogs are the genuine store of value with the most useful carry-forward, because they keep *evolving*. A film catalog is a living asset: rewatched, remade, sampled, licensed, re-released, built upon. It compounds because it stays in motion.

A Banksy edition of twenty-five is a dead asset pretending to be a living one. It was sold as an IP value store when it has no present value even as decoration — which is the cruelest fact in the whole account. The branders sold objects that do not even do the one thing decoration is supposed to do, let alone appreciate. So the asset-class fiction was not merely overpriced. It was pointed at the *wrong medium entirely.* The real generational-wealth carry was sitting in the catalogs the whole time, evolving, locked up by people who made real things — while the extractive capital piled into static decorative objects with manufactured scarcity, because the manufactured-scarcity maneuver was the move that happened to be *legally available* in that medium, and the live value in the catalogs was already owned.

The branders either did not understand this or, far more likely, did not care, because they were going to get theirs. Extraction does not need to understand the asset. It needs the asset to hold its mark long enough to exit.

## VII. The black swan

There was a plan. Replicate the proven Hirst/YBA pump in the street-art prong; address the underclass market; build a constructed keystone identity; run the edition-as-headcount apparatus; carry the never-marked asset class forward on borrowed scarcity for as long as the marks held.

And then "Banksy" turned into a black swan — an event that, instead of executing the plan cleanly, exposed the inside of the fictional-wealth Death Star. The artist's own veto did it: 16/15 on the back of a canvas, the disownment, the exit notation the apparatus could never absorb because absorbing it meant admitting what the edition number actually was. The thing built to be the keystone became the breach.

And the breach can be detonated, now, in a way it could not before. An LLM-mediated information world is the first apparatus that can hold the whole frame in one place at one time: the world-literature-married-to-anglophone-economics theory, the Companies House filings, the auction tape, the catalog raisonné that stops at the year the artist left — all in a single context window, assembled. That is exactly the integration the academy refused to perform when it filed Bataille under literature and ran its equilibrium models in the building next door. So injecting enough toxic-but-true fact into that world is not merely exposure of one venture. It forces a never-before-marked asset class to mark to market — the assembly itself is the detonation — and it does something larger than any single market. It demonstrates the missing variable empirically. It uses the art crash as the worked example of what a civilization does when it has no model for smoothing its own excess.

The Banksy black swan is not the story. It is the proof case for the economic theory the discipline left out.

---

## VIII. The end of the grand game

The grand game was the bet that contemporary art could be financialized into a tradable asset class with brand identity as the underlying — that the artist-as-brand, not the work-as-object, would become the unit of investment, and that this could run forever on marks no one ever publicly tested.

It is ending the way layer one says these things end. Not in a clean collapse the operators control, but in an exposure the operators cannot. The same room that held the manufactured-scarcity objects also held the genuinely scarce ones, and the money chose. The unique original set a record. The reopened edition — whether it took ninety years to fill, like a Brancusi estate cast, or was scripted into a single deal cycle, like a print on canvas dated to a year before its own gallery existed — reverted toward what duplication is actually worth, which is nothing it pretended to be. The catalogs kept evolving, untouched, owned. The desert rocks kept their short true track across the lakebed.

What I do not have yet, and what is ripe to be born, is the plain-English arts-and-letters history that sits underneath all of this — from the formation of the Royal Academy to Y2K — the long account of how the European market refined the valuation of collectable archaic technology over centuries, nailed it, and handed the twentieth century a benchmark record of genuine potency, before the institutions approaching the millennium did what their predecessors had never done: integrated neither the inheritance they were handed nor the thinkers who had already named the missing piece. They ran Marx without Bataille and brand without cauldron, and they got exactly what that produces.

That history is the next thing. This is the spine it grows from.

The grocer's son knew what things cost before he had a theory of price. The theory, when it finally arrived, only confirmed the grammar: you cannot have a billion scarce objects. The arithmetic forbids it. Everything else is just waiting for the room to find out.
