# Setting the Table — Ireland as the Test Site That Bore the Modern Artist

*Opening section. The key term gets defined here — "the Artist," as the word will mean by Y2K — before any later section shows what got sold as its imitation. Not chronological. Organized as the colonial-vacuum pressure-cooker logic: no native system underneath the imposed one, the pressure with nowhere to dissipate, and the singular output it forced.*

---

## The vacuum

England fence-sat the boundary between the trained hand and the commanding mind. Scotland built its useful learning around it. America tried to legislate it away. Ireland is the fourth case, and the instructive one, because in Ireland the boundary was not a domestic class line at all. It was the colonial line, imposed as conquest, with no native system left underneath it to absorb the pressure. That is what makes Ireland the test site. Take a culture, remove its own institutions of learning, install the conqueror's institution in their place as an explicit instrument of rule, lock the native population out of it by law for two centuries, and then watch what the pressure produces when it has nowhere else to go. What it produced, eventually, was the prototype of the thing the twentieth century learned to call the Artist.

Trinity College Dublin was founded by royal charter from Elizabeth I in 1592, and built on the site of the suppressed Catholic Priory of All Hallows. The physical fact is the whole story in miniature: a Protestant university raised on the foundations of a dissolved Catholic monastery. Its purpose was stated without euphemism — it was established to consolidate Tudor rule in Ireland and to be the university of the Protestant Ascendancy, its graduates the civil administrators of the colony. This is the Royal Academy's gatekeeping logic transplanted to a colony and weaponized: the institution that confers status and trains the leadership class, reserved by law for the conquering confession.

The exclusion lasted centuries and unwound in stages that tell you exactly what was being protected. Catholics were barred from enrolling until 1793. After that they could attend but were locked out of the fellowships, professorships, and scholarships — the governing and rewarded positions — until 1873. So even after a Catholic could sit in the room, he could not for another eighty years hold any position of authority within it. And then the inversion that belongs to Ireland alone: in 1871, the moment the institution itself began to drop its bars, the Catholic Church in Ireland imposed its own ban forbidding Catholics to attend Trinity, and did not lift it until 1970. Read the sequence. The colonizer's university excluded Catholics for two centuries; the instant it stopped, the colonized people's own hierarchy excluded its young from entering, because to send Catholic youth into the Ascendancy's university was understood as a threat to the faith. The boundary became self-enforcing from both sides — which is the law of every boundary in this history, stated at maximum pressure: when the gate is legally opened, it reconstitutes itself through whatever authority still wants to keep it shut.

## The institution the excluded built, and the trap it fell into

Because Trinity belonged to the Ascendancy, and because the new non-denominational Queen's Colleges of the 1840s were condemned by the Catholic hierarchy as godless, the excluded population did what excluded populations do in this history: it built its own institution. The Catholic University of Ireland opened in Dublin in 1854, in a Georgian house on St Stephen's Green, with John Henry Newman as its first rector. Newman wrote *The Idea of a University* out of this exact effort — which means the single most influential modern statement in English of what a university is *for*, the noblest articulation of the liberal-humanist ideal of education as the formation of the whole person, was written by the rector of an institution built in defiance of a colonial exclusion, by and for the locked-out. The ideal was articulated by the excluded, not by the secure institutions that supposedly embodied it. Hold that beside Blake denied admission to the Royal Academy. The fused, the excluded, and the most articulate keep turning out to be the same people. That is not sentiment. It is the mechanism: the institution that polices the boundary cannot produce the deepest account of what it is policing, because the account requires the view from outside the gate.

And then the trap, which proves the thesis of the whole work. As a private body, the Catholic University was never granted a royal charter — and so could not award recognized degrees. The state let it teach and would not let it confer. It could train the hand and the mind to any depth and could not stamp the credential, because the credential was the contested thing, not the teaching. It was the same trap, four decades later, that would catch the godless secular college on Gower Street: the establishment will let the threatening institution instruct, but it withholds the legal force of the name "university" — the degree-granting charter — because the name is the weapon. The Catholic University starved on this point until 1880, when the Royal University of Ireland was created as a pure examining-and-degree-awarding body on the University of London model, finally letting its students sit exams and take recognized degrees without attending the Ascendancy's institutions. In 1883 the university, minus its medical school, was handed to the Jesuits and renamed University College. The whole tangle was settled in 1908, when University College became the secular University College Dublin, a constituent college of the new National University of Ireland — and when Belfast's college split off as Queen's University Belfast, drawing the institutional border thirteen years before the politicians drew the political one. The university map prefigured partition. Education is the machine that reproduces the next generation's society, and here it reproduced the coming border before the border existed.

Whatever the faults of the Church and the religion, the Jesuits were the most rigorous educators in the field, and University College is where that rigor met the exclusion. That collision is the pressure cooker. And the output of the collision was James Joyce.

## The output: what the pressure made

Joyce is the prototype, and to see why, you have to hold his case at the right resolution. He was not Trinity. He was University College — the Jesuit workaround institution, the one the excluded built and the state would not let confer. His antagonist-friend of the early years, the Buck Mulligan of *Ulysses*, was Gogarty, the Trinity man, the well-heeled medical student who possessed the enfranchisement Joyce did not. The friction that curdled between them was not personal temperament alone. It was the friction between the man with the credentialed path and the man with the brains and the wrong income, in a country that had decided which of the two it would let modernize it.

That is the taint that drives the whole case: brains plus low income, in a society gated so thoroughly that obvious intellectual capacity could not buy a man a path if he carried the wrong economic mark. Joyce's father had drunk and gambled and whored away the family's standing; the son emerged brilliant and unbacked into a Dublin bourgeoisie that would not print him, would not employ him on terms he could live on, would not give him the break his capacity plainly warranted — because to enfranchise him was to let the wrong sort of person modernize Ireland, and the gate existed precisely to prevent that. So he left, in 1904, and essentially never returned, and wrote everything in exile. The country that would not let him in became the nightmare he wrote his way out of — and the line Stephen Dedalus gives in *Ulysses*, that history is the nightmare from which he is trying to awake, is the colonized man's line exactly: the colonized cannot wake from the history the colonizer wrote, and the university was one room of that nightmare.

Here is the definition the case delivers, and it is the key term for everything downstream. The Artist, as the word comes to mean by the end of the twentieth century, is a person with an aberrant compulsion to produce work in order to push an individual vision — regardless of whether anyone in their own time wants to see it. It is a pathology, in the precise sense: a drive that overrides the ordinary economy of supply and demand, that produces against the market rather than for it, that makes the inexhaustible object no single lifetime can finish perfecting. Blake is the first instance the institutions ever actually had — the working-class engraver fusing poetry and image into the illuminated book, producing the *Four Zoas* and the prophetic works of a span so radical that a thousand-year life would still be perfecting them, and getting for it a passionate engaged life and nothing the market recognized as success. Joyce is the prototype that carries the type into modernity: the one-man production of an object — *Ulysses*, and past it the *Wake* — of such deliberate density that, as he said himself, it would keep the professors busy for centuries. That is the madness of the fine artist in every medium, and it is the thing the later sections will show being counterfeited: the genuine article is the compulsion that makes the inexhaustible work; the counterfeit is the apparatus that manufactures inexhaustibility without the compulsion.

## The maneuver inside the prototype

And this is where Joyce stops being only the genuine article and becomes, as well, the prototype of the *maneuver* — which is why he sets the table so completely. Joyce did not merely make the inexhaustible object. He packaged its interpretation and routed the package through a credentialed intermediary, and in doing so he fired the first version of the move the art market would later run at industrial scale.

The intermediary was Stuart Gilbert, the translator, whose *James Joyce's "Ulysses"* (1930) was written with Joyce himself feeding the schema — the scaffold of Homeric correspondences, the organ and color and technique assigned to each episode, the decoder ring. Joyce authored his own interpretation and handed it to a translator-class reader who could work the text granularly, in close quarters, word by word, and through that reader seeded the academic apparatus that has chased the book for a century since. This is the Banksy move avant la lettre: the maker packaging the authorized reading and placing it through a credentialed channel to manufacture a self-perpetuating apparatus of value around an object the open market would never price on its own terms. The difference — and it is the entire difference between Joyce and the operators the later sections expose — is that Joyce made *both* the inexhaustible thing *and* the decoder. He had earned the apparatus by producing the object that warranted it. The counterfeit version, the one sold through well-educated advertising men in the run-up to 1980, makes only the decoder, and manufactures the scarcity the decoder pretends to explain.

The hardest claim, and it is a claim, not a fact, so it is offered as the stake it is: a century of professors and miles of publication have not produced a single definitive reading that makes a first passage through *Ulysses* anything other than a guided ordeal. The most learned institutions have studied the book for a hundred years without a settled text of understanding emerging from the labor — which leaves a great deal of the scholarship functioning as high-end intellectual tourism, miles of publication that move the aspirant's first journey through the canal no closer to a passage that is not, by design, a tour de force. The book was engineered to be inexhaustible, and the apparatus around it has been, in part, an industry of guides to an object built so that no guide can finish the job. And the *Wake* — which I have not even raised — pushes the same logic to the edge of the readable on purpose. None of this is a charge against Joyce. It is the description of what the genuine article looks like, and of how completely it anticipates the counterfeit: the artist who builds the inexhaustible object and the authorized key to it has invented, in good faith and from genuine compulsion, the exact structure that bad-faith operators will later assemble without the object underneath.

## The table, set

So the table is set with the key term defined twice over. Ireland is the test site: the colonial vacuum, no native system, the exclusion imposed as conquest and then self-enforced from both sides, the excluded building the institution the state would not let confer, the Jesuit rigor meeting the economic taint, and the pressure forcing out a singular output it had no other channel for. And Joyce is the output: the prototype of the Artist as the twentieth century will mean the word — the aberrant compulsion that produces the inexhaustible work against the market, Blake's true heir — and at the same time the prototype of the maneuver, the maker who packages his own difficulty and routes it through a credentialed intermediary to seed a century-long apparatus of value.

Both things are true of one man, which is why he opens the section and defines the term. He is the key and the counterfeit-key in a single figure. Everything that follows — the collectible picture, the manufactured edition, the brand sold by educated ad-men as the YBA bet took shape through the 1970s and locked at 1980 — is the story of what happens when the apparatus is built without the artist underneath it. The genuine article was made in a colonial vacuum by a man the country would not enfranchise. The imitation was sold, two generations later, by the most enfranchised men in the most secure markets on earth. Ireland bore the thing. The metropole learned to fake it. That is the table.
